Shopee Project

Context

Shopee targets users who are ready to make a purchase but are uncertain about which specific product to choose. These users typically have a defined need and intent to buy, but face difficulty navigating large product catalogs, technical specifications, and multiple similar options. Shopee is used at the moment of decision-making, when the user wants guidance to confidently move forward with a purchase.

Product Hypothesis

sers do not want to compare dozens of products or fully understand technical specifications. Instead, they want their needs to be interpreted and translated into a small, confident set of recommendations that clearly fit their intended use.

By reducing choice and framing recommendations around user needs rather than product features, users will feel more confident and move forward with a purchase faster.

MVP Scope Decisions

For the MVP, I deliberately chose not to support product categories where purchasing decisions are primarily subjective or driven by personal taste rather than measurable criteria.

Categories such as clothing were intentionally excluded. While they include fixed attributes (size, material, intended use), the dominant decision factor is personal preference, which cannot be reliably guided through an automated decision assistant.

Given that Shopee’s core hypothesis relies on translating user needs into confident recommendations, including highly subjective categories would have introduced noise into the validation process and weakened the signal. Narrowing the scope allowed the MVP to focus on products where decision criteria are clearer, comparable, and more strongly tied to functional needs.

Decision Flow

Shopee reduces the decision space through a short, structured question flow designed to progressively narrow down user needs.

Instead of presenting a large catalog upfront, the assistant asks a limited set of closed questions (up to six) focused on usage context, constraints, and priorities. Each answer incrementally filters the available catalog based on predefined criteria and available product data.The flow starts with a single question about the user's primary need, such as "I need a new phone" or "I need a new laptop." This question is carefully crafted to elicit a clear, specific need that can be translated into a set of recommendations.

The number of questions was intentionally capped to avoid user fatigue and to preserve momentum toward a decision. The goal is not to fully describe the user, but to collect only the minimum information required to confidently reduce the option space.

Once the filtering process reaches a small and relevant subset, Shopee presents three recommended products. This number was chosen to balance clarity and choice: enough variety to feel in control, but limited enough to prevent decision overload.

Validation & Signals

Shopee was tested through a closed beta with six participants. Each user was given access to the full product catalogs of selected stores, which were also made visible outside the assistant to ensure transparency and avoid any perception of biased or reduced options.

Participants were asked to simulate five purchase decisions each, answering the assistant’s questions differently on every run to generate varied recommendations. For each simulation, users shared both their responses and the three products suggested by Shopee.

Across 30 simulated purchase flows, users reported being satisfied with the recommendations in 18 cases (60%). Satisfaction was defined as receiving options they considered relevant and aligned with their needs, without requiring them to select a final purchase option.

While these results indicate a positive early signal for the decision flow, the validation scope was intentionally limited. The test focused on a single product category, and it did not measure conversion or final purchase behavior. Additional validation would be equired to assess performance across different product types and to evaluate downstream metrics such as click-through or purchase completion.
